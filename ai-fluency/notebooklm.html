   Hands-On: Google Notebook LM 

Goal: Empower learners to use Notebook LM as a learning platform.

NotebookLM is a Google tool that analyzes only the sources you load into each notebook. It’s powerful for aggregating course readings, reports, policies, and research notes and generating multimodal assets from that material.

  • Only upload materials you are allowed to share under campus policies.
  • Avoid highly sensitive data (e.g., confidential HR files, detailed student records).
  • For class work, start with readings, syllabi, slides, datasets, or your own writing.
Tip

Each notebook is siloed: NotebookLM only sees sources inside that notebook, not across all your Google Drive. This makes it well-suited for course- or project-specific research.

How It Works

Sign in to NotebookLM
  1. Open a browser and go to notebooklm.google.com.
  2. Click Sign in and choose the Google account you’ll use for your work (for course or campus work, use your Illinois-associated Google account if available).
  3. Accept any terms or “Labs/experimental” notices. NotebookLM is still evolving, and features may change during the year.

Note: Some institutional Google Workspace configurations may limit access. If you can’t open NotebookLM with your campus account, try a personal Google account with non-sensitive materials only, or check with your local IT contact.

Create a notebook for your project
A notebook is a container for one project or topic. All your sources and AI-generated artifacts for that project live here.
  1. On the left side, click New notebook (or the + tile).
  2. Give it a clear name, such as “AI Ethics Syllabus – Fall 2025” or “Grant Proposal Background Reading.”
  3. Click Create. You’ll see an empty notebook with three main regions: Sources, Chat, and Studio.

Concept check: NotebookLM doesn’t search the open web by default. It works primarily from what you load into the notebook. If something isn’t in your sources, the model may decline to answer or tell you it doesn’t have enough information.

Add sources (your readings, files, and links)
Now give NotebookLM something to work with. Start with 1–5 items so it’s manageable.
  1. In the Sources panel, click Add source (or the + button).
  2. Choose from options such as:
    • PDFs, text, or markdown files from your computer
    • Google Docs or Slides (import from Drive)
    • Web URLs (articles, documentation, reports)
    • YouTube or audio files for lecture-style content
    • Copied & pasted text (for shorter snippets or notes)
  3. Wait while NotebookLM processes each source. You’ll see them listed in the Sources panel.
  4. Click a source name to preview or skim it inside NotebookLM, or open the original in a new tab if you prefer.

Understand the three-panel layout
Most of your work happens in three panels. It’s worth pausing to understand what each one is for before you start clicking buttons.
  • Sources (left): All the files, links, and notes you’ve added to this notebook.
  • Chat (center): A conversation window where you ask questions about your sources, get summaries, and request transformations.
  • Studio (right): A workspace for creating richer artifacts (audio/video overviews, mind maps, study guides, infographics, slide decks, quizzes, flashcards, etc.) from your sources.
Mental model: Think of Chat as “quick questions and drafts” and Studio as “things I might actually keep, share, or present.”
Chat with your sources
Before you touch the Studio tools, it’s useful to see how NotebookLM answers basic questions grounded in your documents.
  1. Click into the Chat panel. NotebookLM will often show a Notebook Guide with a summary and suggested questions.
  2. Try a simple prompt such as:
    • “Give me a 3-paragraph overview of the key arguments across these sources.”
    • “What are three points of disagreement between Source A and Source B?”
    • “Explain the main idea in language a first-year student could understand.”
  3. Skim the answer and scroll down to see which sources and citations it references.
  4. Ask a follow-up that pushes on reasoning or gaps, for example: “What’s missing from this summary that might matter for policy decisions?”
Use it critically: Even though NotebookLM is grounded in your sources, it can still misread nuance or over- simplify. Treat its responses as drafts or hypotheses to check against the originals, not as authoritative answers.
Use the Studio Tools

Now we move into the Studio panel, where NotebookLM turns your sources into more structured learning and presentation artifacts. Each tool is one click to generate, with optional customization panels.

Core Studio Tools Overview

Audio Overview Listen: Generates a podcast-style conversation between AI “hosts” that walks through the key ideas in your sources. Useful for review while commuting or doing other work.

Video Overview Watch: Creates a narrated slideshow with images, quotes, and key points pulled from your documents—like a visual counterpart to the audio overview.

Mind Map & Reports Structure: Generates concept maps, study guides, FAQs, timelines, and briefing docs to organize complex material into digestible chunks.

Infographic & Slide Deck Present: Turns your sources into visual summaries and slide decks, with options to tweak length, language, and emphasis.

Flashcards & Quizzes Practice: Builds question sets from your notebook that you can use for self-testing or share with students for formative review.
