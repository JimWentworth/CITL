Creative Prompting to Generate Images & Video

Image and video generation models respond best to prompts that describe what you want, how it should look, and what to avoid. Creative prompting is less like giving a single command and more like directing a production: you iterate, constrain, and refine.

Key idea: Strong creative prompts specify subject + setting + style + composition + constraints. Then you iterate with small changes rather than rewriting everything.

Creative Prompting Framework (Images & Video)

  • Goal: What is the image/video for? (instructional slide, concept illustration, campus comms, storyboard)
  • Subject: Who/what is in the scene? (objects, people, actions)
  • Setting: Where/when? (environment, era, mood)
  • Style: Photo, illustration, diagram, watercolor, flat icon, cinematic, etc.
  • Composition: Camera angle, framing, focus, background detail, layout space for text
  • Lighting & color: Bright/soft, high contrast, warm/cool palette
  • Constraints: What must be true? What must NOT happen? (no text, no logos, no extra hands)
  • Output specs: Aspect ratio, resolution, duration (video), number of variations

Safety & Professional Use Notes (Worth Saying Out Loud)

  • Consent: Avoid generating realistic depictions of identifiable people without permission.
  • Misleading media: Label synthetic media when used in teaching or communications.
  • Copyright/trademarks: Avoid logos and brand-like imitations in public-facing materials.
  • Accessibility: For instructional images, plan for alt text and clarity (avoid overly busy visuals).

Image Prompting Basics: Start with “Director’s Notes”

Image prompts are strongest when they read like a brief to a designer. Include the instructional goal and the details that matter, then add constraints.

Create an image for: [purpose] SUBJECT: [main subject and action] SETTING: [environment, time of day, mood] STYLE: [photo / illustration / infographic / diagram / watercolor / flat icon] COMPOSITION: [camera angle, framing, background, space for text] CONSTRAINTS: - No text in the image - No logos or trademarks - Keep it simple and uncluttered - Avoid extra limbs/extra fingers if people are present OUTPUT: Aspect ratio: [16:9 / 1:1 / 4:5] Generate: [#] variations

Negative Prompting: “What to Avoid”

Some tools support explicit “negative prompts.” Even when they don’t, you can still include a constraints section that functions the same way.

Avoid: - text overlays, captions, watermarks - logos or brand marks - busy backgrounds - distorted anatomy or extra fingers - unrealistic shadows or reflections - low-resolution or blurry output
Iteration tip: Change one variable at a time (style OR composition OR lighting). Don’t change everything at once.

Video Prompting Basics: Think “Storyboard + Shot List”

Video generation improves dramatically when you specify what happens over time, not just what the scene looks like. Use sequence, camera movement, and duration anchors.

Create a short video. GOAL: [teaching clip / intro bumper / concept visualization] DURATION: [6–10 seconds] SCENE: [where it takes place, mood, time of day] SUBJECT + ACTION: [what the subject does, in order] CAMERA: [static / slow push-in / pan left / overhead] [close-up / medium shot / wide shot] STYLE: [cinematic / documentary / animated / minimalist] CONSTRAINTS: - No readable text - No logos - Avoid flicker and sudden cuts - Keep motion smooth and realistic OUTPUT: Aspect ratio: [16:9 / 9:16] Generate: [#] variations

Creative Iteration: A Simple Improvement Loop

  • Round 1: Generate 3–4 variations with clear constraints.
  • Round 2: Pick one and refine only composition (camera angle, framing, background).
  • Round 3: Refine style/lighting (more contrast, simpler palette, cleaner lines).
  • Round 4: Add “delivery” constraints (space for text, consistent visual language across slides).

Conceptual Wrap-Up

Creative prompting is direction plus constraint. The more clearly you define what matters—and what must be avoided— the more usable your images and videos become for teaching and communication.

Show Expanded Explanation

Image/video models often “help” by inventing details, adding text-like artifacts, or making composition choices you didn’t ask for. Prompts that include explicit constraints (no text, no logos, simple background) and structured production notes (camera, duration, motion) reduce unwanted surprises and speed up iteration.

Practical Guidance for Teaching and Academic Work

For classroom visuals, prioritize clarity over “wow.” Use simple compositions, high contrast, and minimal clutter. If the media supports a learning objective, ask: “What should a student be able to explain after seeing this?”

If you plan to share media publicly, avoid realistic depictions of identifiable people and avoid brand/trademark elements. When in doubt, keep outputs abstract, illustrative, or diagrammatic.
