Faculty, Students, and Academic Integrity in the Age of AI

Generative AI has changed how students approach coursework. For faculty, the challenge is not simply identifying AI use, but establishing clear expectations that support learning, fairness, and trust.

Start with Clarity, Not Assumptions

Students bring widely varying assumptions about what is allowed when using AI. Some assume everything is prohibited; others assume everything is permitted.

Academic integrity is best supported when faculty clearly communicate:

  • When AI use is allowed, limited, or not permitted
  • What kinds of assistance are acceptable (brainstorming, editing, checking)
  • What responsibilities remain with the student
Key idea: Ambiguity about AI use increases misuse. Clarity reduces it.

Frame AI as a Tool, Not a Shortcut

Students are more likely to engage responsibly with AI when it is framed as a tool that supports learning rather than a way to bypass it.

  • Explain how AI can help with practice, feedback, or revision
  • Emphasize that understanding and judgment cannot be outsourced
  • Reinforce that students remain accountable for accuracy and reasoning

Use Assignment Design to Reinforce Integrity

Many integrity concerns can be addressed through design rather than enforcement.

  • Include reflection or process explanations
  • Require connections to course-specific material
  • Break large assignments into stages
Example requirement:
“Briefly describe how you approached this assignment, including any tools or resources you used and why.”

Responding When AI Use Is Unclear

When faculty suspect inappropriate AI use, the goal should be understanding before escalation.

  • Ask students to explain their thinking or choices
  • Focus on learning gaps rather than tool use
  • Document expectations and conversations

In many cases, a conversation clarifies misunderstanding and reinforces expectations more effectively than punitive action.

Teaching Students to Disclose AI Use

Disclosure normalizes responsible use and discourages concealment.

  • Allow brief AI-use statements or footnotes
  • Clarify that disclosure is about transparency, not punishment
  • Model disclosure in your own materials
Example disclosure language for students:
“I used an AI tool to brainstorm initial ideas and revise wording. All analysis and final decisions are my own.”

Conceptual Wrap-Up

Academic integrity in an AI-enabled classroom is a shared responsibility. Faculty set the conditions; students practice judgment within them.

Show Expanded Explanation

When integrity is framed solely as rule enforcement, students may focus on hiding tool use rather than learning how to use tools well. Transparent expectations and reflective design shift attention back to learning.

Over time, these practices help students develop ethical habits that extend beyond a single course.

Practical Guidance for Teaching and Academic Work

Revisit AI expectations at multiple points in the term, not just in the syllabus.

Use consistent language across assignments and feedback.

Treat integrity conversations as instructional moments whenever possible.
