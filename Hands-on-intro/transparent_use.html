Designing AI-Transparent Assignments

AI-transparent assignments make expectations about AI use explicit and treat AI as a visible part of the learning process. Rather than trying to prevent AI use, these assignments focus on how students think, decide, and learn—whether or not AI tools are involved.

What “AI-Transparent” Means

An AI-transparent assignment clearly communicates:

  • Whether AI use is allowed, limited, or expected
  • What kinds of AI assistance are acceptable
  • What responsibilities remain with the student
  • How AI use should be disclosed

Transparency reduces confusion, discourages hidden use, and aligns assessment with learning goals.

Key idea: When AI use is invisible, integrity becomes a guessing game. When it is explicit, integrity becomes a design feature.

Start with the Learning, Not the Tool

Before deciding how AI fits, clarify what you want students to practice or demonstrate. Ask:

  • What thinking or judgment should students develop?
  • Which parts of the task matter most for learning?
  • Where could AI support learning without replacing it?

AI-transparent assignments protect the most important intellectual work.

Common AI-Transparent Design Patterns

  • Process + product: students submit both their work and a brief explanation of how they approached it.
  • Draft + reflection: students revise after feedback and explain what changed.
  • Source-grounded tasks: students must connect claims to course materials.
  • Evaluation prompts: AI is used to critique, not generate, final answers.

Example: Making a Traditional Assignment AI-Transparent

Traditional version:
“Write a 4–5 page paper explaining concept X.”
AI-transparent version:
  • Draft an explanation of concept X in your own words.
  • Use AI (if desired) to review clarity or organization.
  • Revise your draft.
  • Submit a short reflection (200 words) describing:
    • how you used AI (if at all), and
    • what decisions you made during revision.

Language You Can Reuse in Assignments

You may use AI tools for brainstorming, feedback, or revision. You are responsible for the accuracy, reasoning, and final content of your submission. If you use AI, include a brief note explaining: - what tool you used, - how you used it, and - what you changed based on your own judgment.

What to Assess Instead of “AI Use”

AI-transparent assignments shift assessment away from tool detection and toward learning evidence.

  • Quality of reasoning and explanation
  • Use of course concepts or sources
  • Clarity of decision-making
  • Reflection on strengths and limitations

Conceptual Wrap-Up

AI-transparent assignments acknowledge reality: students have access to AI. Good design ensures that access supports learning rather than undermines it.

Show Expanded Explanation

When assignments are designed around thinking, context, and reflection, AI becomes a tool students can engage with responsibly. Transparency reduces fear on both sides and makes expectations visible.

Over time, these assignments help students develop transferable skills for working with AI beyond the classroom.

Practical Guidance for Teaching and Academic Work

Start by making one assignment AI-transparent rather than redesigning everything.

Share example disclosures or reflections so students know what “good” looks like.

Revisit assignment language each term as tools and norms evolve.
