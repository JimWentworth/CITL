Multimodal Prompting Basics (Images + Video)

Multimodal AI can interpret images and videos, but it still follows the same rule as text-only AI: it predicts what to say next based on patterns. Strong multimodal prompting helps the model separate what is visible from what is inferred, and keeps outputs aligned with your purpose (teaching, accessibility, analysis, or verification).

Key idea: The fastest way to improve multimodal results is to (1) state the task, (2) give context, (3) set boundaries, and (4) ask for structured output.

Multimodal Prompting Framework

  • Task: What do you want the model to do with the media?
  • Context: What course, audience, or learning goal matters?
  • Boundaries: What should it avoid guessing?
  • Structure: What format should the response use?
One-line starter:
“Analyze this image for a teaching purpose. Separate what is visible from what is inferred, and present your output in a structured format.”

Images: The Most Reliable Prompt Pattern

For images, you’ll get better results if you explicitly separate:

  • Describe: what is literally visible (objects, text, layout)
  • Interpret: what it might mean (with uncertainty labels)
  • Use: what you want to do next (alt text, teaching questions, critique)
You are analyzing an image for educational use. 1) DESCRIPTION (only what is visible; do not infer intent) 2) INFERENCES (clearly label what is inferred vs. visible) 3) QUESTIONS (3 questions a student should be able to answer about this image) 4) RISKS (anything ambiguous or easy to misread) Audience: [e.g., first-year undergraduates] Course context: [e.g., biology lab / history seminar / stats course]

Images: Prompts for Common Teaching Tasks

Alt text (accessibility):
Write alt text for this image. INSTRUCTIONAL PURPOSE: [What should learners get from this image?] RULES: - 1–2 sentences unless it’s a data-heavy chart - Include text shown in the image if it matters - Do not add interpretation that isn’t visible
Chart/graph interpretation:
Explain what this chart shows. OUTPUT: - Identify axes and variables (from the image) - Summarize the main trend(s) - Note any outliers or anomalies - List 2 possible misinterpretations students might make - Provide 2 discussion questions Do not invent numbers not shown.
Diagram critique (clarity and labeling):
Act as a student encountering this diagram for the first time. 1) What is immediately clear? 2) What is confusing or under-labeled? 3) Suggest 3 improvements to labels, layout, or sequencing. Do not change the content—only presentation.

Video: Prompting Works Best With “Anchors”

Video is harder than images because it contains time, motion, and many possible focal points. Strong video prompts use anchors:

  • Time anchors: “Focus on 00:45–01:20”
  • Scene anchors: “When the speaker draws the triangle”
  • Goal anchors: “Extract the steps of the process”
Analyze this video for a teaching purpose. GOAL: [Summarize / extract steps / identify misconceptions / create study questions] ANCHOR: Focus on: [time range OR scene description] OUTPUT: 1) What happens (objective summary) 2) Key terms or concepts used 3) Steps or sequence (if relevant) 4) 3 quiz questions with answers 5) Anything uncertain or not visible/audible Do not guess missing details. If unclear, say so.

Video: High-Value Classroom Outputs

Turn a video segment into a study guide:
Create a short study guide from the anchored segment of this video. Include: - 5 bullet summary - glossary of 5 terms (plain language + technical definition) - 3 “check your understanding” questions - 1 common misconception and correction Anchor: [time/scene] Audience: [course level]
Identify likely confusion points (student perspective):
Assume you are a student watching this segment. 1) List 3 points that might confuse a student and why. 2) For each, suggest a clarifying sentence or quick example the instructor could add. Anchor: [time/scene]

Verification: How to Reduce Multimodal Hallucinations

Multimodal models can misread blurry text, assume context, or “fill in” missing detail. Use prompts that force carefulness:

  • Require separation: “Visible vs. inferred”
  • Require uncertainty labels: “Certain / likely / unclear”
  • Constrain claims: “Do not invent numbers or quotes”
  • Ask for checks: “What would you need to confirm?”
Before you answer, do a reliability pass. 1) List what you are confident is visible/audible. 2) List what is uncertain or ambiguous. 3) Ask me 2 clarification questions you would need to answer accurately. Then provide the best-effort answer with uncertainty clearly marked.

Conceptual Wrap-Up

Multimodal prompting is mostly about controlling attention and controlling inference. When you define the task, provide anchors, and require structured outputs, you get more reliable results—and better teaching materials.

Show Expanded Explanation

Images and video add ambiguity: blurred text, missing context, multiple focal points, and non-verbal meaning. Models will often “help” by guessing. Prompts that require visible/inferred separation and uncertainty labeling reduce that guessing and make outputs easier to trust.

Practical Guidance for Teaching and Academic Work

Start with small, concrete tasks: alt text for one image, quiz questions from a 30-second video clip, or a chart interpretation guide. Use anchored prompts and structured outputs.

If the output will be shared with students, do a quick verification pass: check the chart axes, confirm any quoted text, and remove any invented details.
