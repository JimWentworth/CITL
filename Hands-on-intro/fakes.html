Hands-On Introduction to Generative AI

Spotting AI-Generated Images and Videos

As generative AI tools become more accessible, synthetic images and videos—often called “AI fakes” or “deepfakes”—are increasingly common in academic, professional, and public contexts. Developing the ability to critically evaluate visual media is now a core component of AI literacy.

First Principle: There Is No Single “Tell”

AI-generated images and videos are created by probabilistic models trained on massive collections of visual data. Because the technology evolves quickly, there is no permanent checklist that reliably identifies every AI fake.

Instead of looking for one giveaway detail, effective detection relies on combining multiple signals: visual artifacts, contextual inconsistencies, and source verification.

Common Signs in AI-Generated Images

  • Anatomical inconsistencies: extra or misshapen fingers, asymmetrical eyes, distorted ears, or unnatural joint angles.
  • Texture and detail errors: smeared text, unreadable signage, repeating background patterns, or inconsistent lighting.
  • Boundary confusion: objects blending into one another or unclear edges between foreground and background.
  • Style mismatch: highly realistic subjects combined with painterly or overly smooth textures.

These issues occur because image models optimize for visual plausibility rather than physical correctness.

Common Signs in AI-Generated Videos

  • Temporal instability: faces, hands, or objects subtly changing shape from frame to frame.
  • Unnatural motion: stiff facial expressions, limited blinking, or movements that feel slightly delayed or smoothed.
  • Audio-visual mismatch: lip movements that don’t precisely align with speech, or vocal tone that lacks natural variation.
  • Background drift: walls, shadows, or objects subtly shifting over time.
Key idea: AI fakes often look convincing at a glance. Detection improves when viewers slow down, rewatch, zoom in, and consider context—not just visual quality.

Contextual and Source-Based Red Flags

  • Missing provenance: no clear source, creator, or original publication.
  • Improbable scenarios: events that would likely be widely reported elsewhere but appear only in a single clip.
  • Emotional manipulation: content designed to provoke outrage, fear, or urgency without supporting evidence.
  • Metadata gaps: stripped or inconsistent metadata in image or video files.

Conceptual Wrap-Up

AI-generated visuals are best understood as statistically plausible reconstructions rather than recordings of real events. They prioritize what looks right over what actually happened.

Show Expanded Explanation

Image and video generation models predict pixel values and motion patterns based on probability. This means they are excellent at reproducing familiar visual motifs, but weaker at enforcing physical laws, continuity, and causal history.

As models improve, surface-level artifacts will become harder to spot. Long-term detection strategies will increasingly depend on contextual literacy, verification, and understanding how generative systems work.

Practical Guidance for Teaching and Academic Work

When using images or videos in teaching materials, encourage students to ask: Who created this? Where did it come from? What evidence supports it?

Consider classroom activities where students compare real and AI-generated visuals, document observed artifacts, and reflect on uncertainty rather than aiming for perfect classification.

Framing AI fakes as a literacy challenge—not a guessing game—helps students build durable critical skills.
